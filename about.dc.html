VISION · OBJECTIVES · CO-CREATION

Restore what can be restored.

If you came this far, you're interested in not only restoring, but improving ecosystems — and in the science of how a living landscape holds together.

Since farming was invented, human beings have been catapulted tangentially out of the ecosystem circularity and stability it once enjoyed.

In complex-systems science, a resilient system is one capable of recovering from disturbance, while an adaptable system can change to become resistant to it.

We have come to believe that the hunter-gatherer period was characteristic of a net-positive natural-capital production system. Humans were dependent on natural resources for the same things we need today — shelter, food, fiber, fodder and medicine.

"I can change."
ONE NEUROBIOCHEMICAL REACTION · A TRAJECTORY-DEFINING THOUGHT

One day, or night, that thought sparked. We have been changing the environment to our needs ever since — slowly, then faster.

If that temporal scale is hard to picture, you can see it illustrated in Joe Bennett's Emmy-winning Scavenger's Reign: a balanced world is destabilized when a single node in the network begins overexploiting — using or hoarding — resources. As an avid wildlife enthusiast, I'd say the animation is like discovering new biomes on Earth, each with its own balances and checks, and watching them collapse around you.

A baseline worth returning to: the Cantabrian Mixed Forests before agriculture.
MISSION

Assess, monitor, manage, replicate and report on complex and adaptive ecosystems.

OBJECTIVE

To co-create, with a community of wildlife enthusiasts and experts, knowledge on biological systems and their complex interactions — advancing the science of ecosystem connectivity. As design engineers, but also nodes in the system, motivation matters: the more ideas the community contributes, the faster we move.

THE OUTCOME

As homo sapiens — owners of the capacity for change — we direct our efforts to first restore what can be restored of a landscape's natural capital, taking the pre-agricultural, hunter-gatherer period as a baseline. The first outcome is to co-create knowledge on the ecosystem network for the Cantabrian Mixed Forests.
