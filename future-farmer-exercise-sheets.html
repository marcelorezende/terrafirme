Teacher Notes  ·  Science: Meet Your Crop
About This Sheet

This sheet works well as a pre-game warm-up activity. Students benefit from choosing their own crops as it increases engagement. Allow 20 minutes for research, 10 minutes for sharing, then move directly into a game round. Works well with science curriculum units on plants, agriculture, and ecosystems.

Teaching Tips
  • Pair students who chose crops from different continents — good for geography cross-links.
  • If internet access is limited, prepare a printed "crop fact sheet" with 6–8 crops.
  • The sharing step doubles as a low-stakes speaking exercise.
Answer Guidance
  • Activity 2 has no single correct answer — look for accuracy and appropriate level of detail.
  • Activity 3: accept any reasonable connection; the quality of the explanation matters more than the card chosen.
  • Reflection 2: look for evidence that students are thinking beyond the rules (e.g. "I protected my wheat because Drought was in the discard pile").

Teacher Notes  ·  Environmental Studies: When Challenges Strike
About This Sheet

This sheet supports environmental studies units on climate change, food security, and sustainability. It works well after students have played at least one full game round so they understand how Challenge cards feel in play. The pause-and-discuss approach (playing, then stopping when a Challenge card appears) is particularly effective.

Teaching Tips
  • Assign each student a different Challenge card to prevent duplication and allow a richer class discussion.
  • Activity 3 can be done on a separate piece of paper and attached to the sheet.
  • This sheet pairs well with the Science "Meet Your Crop" sheet — students can investigate how their chosen crop is affected by their chosen Challenge.
Answer Guidance
  • Activity 2: look for a specific, real event with a named location and time period — not general statements.
  • Activity 3: a good diagram will show at least two steps of consequence (not just crop loss, but knock-on effects on communities or economies).
  • Activity 4: acceptable strategies include irrigation improvements, crop rotation, drought-resistant varieties, insurance schemes, community seed banks, etc.

Teacher Notes  ·  Mathematics & Strategy: Farm by Numbers
About This Sheet

This sheet reinforces KS3 mathematics skills: arithmetic, ratio, and data interpretation. It works best when completed during or immediately after a game round. Students who struggle with the efficiency ratio may benefit from a worked example on the board first. High-ability students can be challenged to calculate the probability of a Challenge card appearing in a given round.

Teaching Tips
  • Activity 1 can be set up before the game starts so students track in real time.
  • Pair students for Activity 2 so they can compare each other's data directly.
  • Activity 3 connects to the specific wording on the Drought card ("worth 3 or fewer credits") — good for literal comprehension of rules.
  • Extension: challenge students to calculate the percentage difference between efficiency ratios.
Answer Guidance
  • Activity 2: ensure students are dividing correctly — watch for "credits × rounds" errors.
  • Activity 3: a many-cheap-cards strategy is generally more vulnerable to challenges that destroy all modules below a threshold.
  • Reflection 1: look for understanding of trade-offs (high-value cards give more points but are riskier targets for Challenges).

Teacher Notes  ·  Design Thinking: Design a Card
About This Sheet

This sheet aligns with design technology and creative arts curricula. It deliberately mirrors a professional card game design process. The peer feedback step (Activity 4) is crucial — encourage students to be specific rather than vague. As an extension, test the new cards in an actual game round and let the class vote on which card best fits the game's theme and balance.

Teaching Tips
  • Prepare a one-page "existing cards reference list" so students know what's already in the game.
  • Remind students that real games go through dozens of revisions — the feedback step isn't criticism, it's improvement.
  • For a longer project, this activity can span two lessons: design in lesson 1, playtest in lesson 2.
  • The "Define / Ideate / Prototype / Test" labels in each activity heading mirror the design thinking framework explicitly.
Answer Guidance
  • Activity 2: a credit value of 1–3 suggests a basic/utility card; 4–6 mid-range; 7–10 powerful/rare. Guide students to justify their choice.
  • Activity 4: check that feedback is specific — "I like the drawing" is not helpful; "the effect is unclear because…" is.
  • There are no wrong designs, but good designs will show the student has considered both the real-world concept and game balance.

Introduction

During a game of The Future Farmer, you will face moments where you can help another player — or hurt them. These are not just game decisions. Teacher Notes  ·  Ethics & Society: Fair Farm or Every Farmer for Themselves?
About This Sheet

This sheet works particularly well as a post-game debrief. It supports PSHE, citizenship, and humanities curricula. Activity 4 can be extended into a structured class debate. Ensure that the real-world examples in Activity 3 are treated sensitively — students may have personal connections to food insecurity or land rights issues.

Teaching Tips
  • Before the game, tell students they will be completing this sheet afterwards — it encourages them to notice ethical decisions as they happen.
  • Activity 3 benefits from brief teacher-led examples before students write independently.
  • The debate in Activity 4 can be run as a fishbowl discussion (small inner group debates, outer group observes and scores arguments).
Answer Guidance
  • Activity 1: any genuine game moment qualifies — there are no wrong answers here.
  • Activity 2: look for students acknowledging that both choices have legitimate reasoning, not just that one is obviously right.
  • Reflection 1: strong answers will engage with both the fun/realism dimensions rather than answering only one.

The Future Farm Report

Art · Geography · Writing — one project that brings it all together
THE FUTURE FARMER
Years 7–9  ·  2–3 lessons
Name: ___________________________________ Date: ________________ Class: ________________
Learning Objectives
  • Apply knowledge from science, geography, and creative writing in a single project.
  • Research a local or traditional farming practice and represent it creatively.
  • Communicate findings through multiple formats (map, illustration, written report).
What You'll Need

The Future Farmer Card Game (for inspiration and reference)  ·  Internet or library access  ·  Blank paper for illustration  ·  This worksheet

Introduction

This project brings together everything you have explored in The Future Farmer lessons. You will choose a local, traditional, or indigenous farming practice, investigate it across three subject lenses — Geography, Art, and Creative Writing — and produce a short Future Farm Report to present to the class.

Activities
Geography Activity 1 — Map It

Choose a farming practice from anywhere in the world that connects to the game's themes. On a blank page, draw or trace a simple map showing: where the practice is used, the climate, what is grown/raised, and one Challenge this region commonly faces. Attach your map to this worksheet.

Writing Activity 3 — A Farmer's Story

Write a short story (150–250 words) told from the perspective of a farmer using your chosen practice. Include at least one moment where a Challenge (from the game) affects their farm.

Teacher Notes  ·  Cross-Subject Project: The Future Farm Report
About This Sheet

This multi-lesson project is designed to be the capstone activity for a Future Farmer classroom unit. It explicitly requires cross-subject skills: geographical research and cartography, visual art and card design, and narrative creative writing. It can be assessed against multiple subject objectives simultaneously. Presentations can be informal (share with a partner) or formal (class gallery walk with cards displayed).

Suggested Lesson Plan
  • Lesson 1: Introduce the project, students choose their farming practice and complete Activity 1 (map).
  • Lesson 2: Activities 2 and 3 — allow students to work at their own pace.
  • Lesson 3: Presentations and class discussion.
  • Consider inviting a local farmer or environmental educator to hear the presentations.
Answer Guidance
  • Activity 1: look for maps that show accurate geographic context, not just a rough sketch.
  • Activity 2: assess card design on clarity and thematic fit, not artistic skill.
  • Activity 3: strong stories will show cause and effect clearly and demonstrate empathy for the farmer's perspective.
  • Reflection 2: personal reflections are valid — what matters is evidence of genuine engagement with the theme.
